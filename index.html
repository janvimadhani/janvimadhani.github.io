EverestK2 lightcurve de-trending
EVEREST (EPIC Variability Extraction and Removal for Exoplanet Science Targets) is an open-source pipeline for removing instrumental noise from light curves generated by K2, the repurposed Kepler spacecraft. Operating with only two of four reaction wheels, K2 is unable to maintain the precise pointing precision required to detect the small light modulations caused by transiting exoplanets. EVEREST exploits correlations across the pixels on the Kepler CCD to remove systematics introduced by the spacecraft's pointing error, yielding light curves with precision comparable to that of the original Kepler mission. EVEREST was coded in Python and is freely available on github.

PysyzygyPython transit code
A fast, flexible, and user-friendly exoplanet transit code for the general case of a massive, eccentric planet with transit timing variations. Written in C for speed and wrapped in Python for ease of use. Based on the Mandel and Agol (2002) transit model.

Pale Green DotAurorae on Proxima b
The recently-discovered planet orbiting the Sun's closest neighbor, Proxima Centauri, sits comfortably in its star's habitable zone, the region where the presence of liquid water on a planet's surface could give life a shot at evolving. However, because Proxima Centauri is a magnetically active low-mass star, its planet is constantly bombarded by extremely high energy stellar wind. On Earth, interactions of the solar wind with our magnetic field cause the aurora, commonly known as the Northern (or Southern) Lights. On Proxima Centauri b, provided this planet is terrestrial, aurorae may be one hundred to ten thousand times stronger, bathing the planet in an iridescent green glow. In a recent paper submitted to ApJ, my colleagues and I have shown that this signal may be detectable by upcoming telescopes, providing a powerful means to characterize the orbit and atmospheric composition of the closest habitable zone planet to Earth.

About meAbout Rodrigo
VPLANET is my advisor Rory Barnes' planet evolution simulator, which we recently used to assess the habitability of Proxima Centauri b (see links below). The goal of VPLANET is to model the evolution of potentially habitable planets from their formation to the present day, accounting for processes such as stellar evolution, atmospheric escape, volcanic outgassing, climate evolution, planet tectonics and mantle processes, radiogenic decay in the core, etc. VPLANET is still under development, but we hope to release the code to the public soon.

Mirage EarthsM Dwarf water loss
Low mass M dwarf stars have recently become popular targets for planet searches, particularly those aiming to find potentially habitable Earth-size planets. Not only are M dwarfs the most numerous stars in the galaxy, but their low luminosities mean that their habitable zone is much closer in, making such planets easier to find around these stars. However, in a recent Astrobiology article, I found that the habitability of many planets around M dwarfs may be compromised by processes that happen early on, shortly after their formation. These stars spend their first several hundred million years in a superluminous phase with vigorous magnetic activity, blasting any HZ planets with enough high energy radiation to vaporize their surface water and cause it to escape to space. In addition to losing the equivalent of many Earth oceans of water, these planets may also build up large amounts of oxygen from the photolysis of their water. This is important since we typically think of oxygen as a biosignature gas -- that is, a sign that life may be present, as it is on Earth. However, this is certainly not true for many planets orbiting M dwarfs, as the evolution of life on these planets may have been stunted early on.

HECsHabitable evaporated cores
In a paper published in Astrobiology, I showed that tidal forces and stellar activity (generally regarded as "bad" for habitability) can sometimes improve the chances that an exoplanet is habitable. Under the right circumstances, tidal evolution and atmospheric escape can shape planets that start out as "mini-Neptunes" (planets larger than the Earth with solid cores and thick hydrogen envelopes) into gas-free, potentially habitable worlds. Tidal forces can help mini-Neptunes to migrate into the habitable zone of M dwarf stars, where early on they are exposed to high levels of X-ray and ultraviolet radiation. These, in turn, can lead to rapid loss of the hydrogen and helium to space, in some cases leaving behind a hydrogen-free, terrestrial world in the habitable zone, which I call a "habitable evaporated core" (HEC). Such a planet is likely to have abundant surface water and could thus be habitable. Check out the links below for more!
